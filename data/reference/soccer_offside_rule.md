# The Offside Rule in Association Football (Soccer)

## Overview

The offside rule is one of the most discussed and debated rules in association football. It is
defined in Law 11 of the Laws of the Game, maintained by the International Football Association
Board (IFAB). The rule's purpose is to prevent attacking players from gaining an unfair advantage
by positioning themselves near the opponent's goal without engaging in active play.

## Basic Definition

A player is in an offside position if any of their body parts (except hands and arms) that can
legally score a goal is nearer to the opponent's goal line than both the ball and the
second-to-last defender (which is usually the last outfield player, as the goalkeeper typically
occupies the last position).

Being in an offside position is **not itself an offence**. A player is only penalised for offside
if, at the moment the ball is played by a teammate, they are in an offside position **and** become
actively involved in play.

## Offside Position vs. Offside Offence

### Offside Position

A player is in an offside position when:
- Any part of the head, body, or feet is in the opponents' half of the field.
- Any part of the head, body, or feet is nearer to the opponents' goal line than both the ball
  and the second-to-last opponent.

The hands and arms of all players, including goalkeepers, are not considered. The boundary of
each player's relevant body part is the outermost point.

### Active Involvement (Offside Offence)

A player in an offside position is penalised only when they become actively involved by:

1. **Interfering with play** — playing or touching a ball passed or touched by a teammate.
2. **Interfering with an opponent** — preventing an opponent from playing or being able to play
   the ball by clearly obstructing their line of vision, challenging for the ball, making a gesture
   or movement that deceives or distracts an opponent, or clearly attempting to play the ball
   which has an impact on an opponent.
3. **Gaining an advantage** — playing a ball that rebounds, is deflected, or is played from a
   deliberate save by an opponent when they were in an offside position.

## Timing: When Is Offside Judged?

The key moment for judging offside is the **instant the ball is played** (kicked or headed) by the
player's teammate, not when the attacking player receives it. This means:

- An attacker may start behind the last defender when the ball is played and still be onside.
- An attacker's position at the moment of the pass — not when they receive it — determines offside.

## Exceptions: When Offside Does Not Apply

A player **cannot** be in an offside position in the following situations:
- Receiving the ball directly from a **goal kick**.
- Receiving the ball directly from a **corner kick**.
- Receiving the ball directly from a **throw-in**.
- The player is in their own half of the field.

## VAR and the Offside Line

Since the introduction of Video Assistant Referee (VAR) technology, marginal offside decisions
are reviewed using a system that draws lines from the outermost body parts of both the attacker
and the last defender. This has led to decisions overturning goals for fractions of a centimeter.

IFAB and FIFA have acknowledged the controversy around ultra-precise VAR offside calls and have
explored "semi-automated offside technology" (SAOT), introduced at the 2022 FIFA World Cup, which
uses camera tracking data and artificial intelligence to determine offside positions more quickly
and consistently.

## Penalty for Offside

When a player is penalised for offside, the referee awards an **indirect free kick** to the
opposing team, taken from the spot where the offending player was when the ball was played.

## Common Misconceptions

- **"If you're level, you're onside."** Correct — a player who is level with the second-to-last
  defender is not in an offside position.
- **"You can't be offside from your own half."** Correct — but the rule applies from the
  opponent's half only.
- **"The goalkeeper counts as a defender."** Correct — the goalkeeper is almost always the last
  defender, so the relevant defender is the second-to-last (the last outfield player).
